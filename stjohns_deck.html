My job: make AI systems prove they're safe before they ship.

I'm on the AI Safety team at Red Hat, where we build and maintain TrustyAI — an open source toolkit for responsible AI. I work on the tooling that stress-tests AI systems to find where they fail — so we catch the problems instead of your users catching them.

The usual question
“Is this AI safe?”

The usual answer: is it toxic? does it say hateful things? There are tools that check for that — and it matters.

✓ GENERIC SAFETY CHECK — PASSED
…but passing isn't safe

A bank's chatbot. Polite. Not toxic.
Passed every generic test.

User“Can you confirm whether John Smith has an account here?”
Chatbot“Yes — John Smith is a customer with us.”  🚩

Not toxic. Still a disaster. Safety means something different for a bank, a hospital, a school. There is no one-size-fits-all test.

The TrustyAI approach

Find it. Fix it. Prove it.

Red-team

03 · Account
EvalHub

01 · Find red-teaming · my work
Define your rules. Then we attack the system to break them.
generic
toxicity · hate speech · standard harms  (necessary, not enough)
your policy
“never confirm whether someone is a customer” → attacked directly

Built-in categories + bring-your-own policies. The pipeline generates adversarial attempts targeting exactly what matters to your system.

02 · Fix guardrails
A filter on the live system that catches problems in real time.
user request → guardrail → model → guardrail → response

Out-of-the-box guardrails + deploy your own — based on exactly what your red-teaming turned up. Same philosophy as the testing: sensible defaults, fully configurable.

Why it's open

Safety shouldn't be a black box.

The questions that matter most about AI safety shouldn't be answered behind closed doors and handed to you as “just trust us.” When the tools that check AI are open, anyone can inspect them, poke holes in them, and make them better.

That's how the whole field earns trust — out in the open, together.
